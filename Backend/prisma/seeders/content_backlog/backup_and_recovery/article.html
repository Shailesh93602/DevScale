Database Backup and Recovery Strategies

Backup and recovery are critical for ensuring data durability and business continuity in the event of hardware failure, database corruption, or accidental deletion.

1. Types of Backups

  • Full Backup: A complete copy of the entire database. This is usually done daily or weekly.
  • Incremental Backup: Only the changes made since the last backup (full or incremental) are saved. This is faster and takes less storage.
  • Differential Backup: Only the changes since the last full backup are saved. It's faster than a full backup but larger than an incremental one.
  • Point-in-Time Recovery (PITR): Allows you to restore your database to a specific second or transaction. This is often achieved using write-ahead logs (WAL).

2. Backup Methods

  • Physical Backups: Copying the actual data files directly. This is very fast but requires the database version to be consistent between backup and restore.
  • Logical Backups (SQL Dump): Exporting the data as a series of SQL commands (e.g., mysqldump, pg_dump). This is slower but more flexible and database-version independent.

3. Recovery Time and Point Objectives

  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective): The maximum acceptable time to restore the database after a failure.
  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective): The maximum acceptable amount of data loss, usually measured in time (e.g., 'we can't lose more than 15 minutes of transactions').

4. Best Practices

  • Offsite Storage: Always store backups in a different geographical location or a different cloud provider.
  • Regular Testing: A backup that hasn't been tested for restoration is not a backup. Regularly test your recovery procedures.
  • Automation: Automate your backup schedule to avoid human error.

Conclusion

Choosing the right backup strategy involves balancing storage costs, performance impact, and your business's data durability requirements. Implement a solid backup and recovery plan today to protect your application's most valuable asset: its data.
