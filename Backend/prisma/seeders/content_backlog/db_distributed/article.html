Understanding Distributed Databases

A distributed database is a database in which storage devices are not all attached to a common processor. It may be stored in multiple computers, located in the same physical location or may be dispersed over a network of interconnected computers.

1. Homogeneous vs. Heterogeneous

  • Homogeneous: All nodes use the same hardware and the same DBMS. They appear to the user as a single system.
  • Heterogeneous: Nodes may have different hardware, different operating systems, and even different DBMSs. They communicate through global schemas.

2. Replication vs. Fragmentation

Replication involves storing copies of the same data at multiple locations. Fragmentation involves breaking the database into small pieces (fragments) and storing them at different locations where they are most frequently used.

3. Key Benefits

Distributed databases provide high availability, improved performance through local access, and modular growth. If one node fails, the rest of the system can continue to operate.

Conclusion

In the age of global applications, distributed databases are essential for providing low-latency access to users regardless of their physical location.
