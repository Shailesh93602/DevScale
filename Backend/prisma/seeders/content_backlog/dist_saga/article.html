The Saga Pattern: Distributed Transactions

In a microservices architecture, a single business transaction (like "Order Food") might involve 5 different services. If the "Payment Service" fails, how do you undo the "Order Service"? The Saga pattern is the solution.

1. What is a Saga?

A Saga is a sequence of local transactions. Each service performs its own work and publishes an event or message to trigger the next service. If one step fails, the Saga runs **Compensating Transactions** to undo the previous steps (e.g., "Refund Money", "Cancel Order").

2. Choreography vs. Orchestration

  • Choreography: Every service knows which event to listen to and what to do next. It's decentralized and simple but can become hard to track as the system grows.
  • Orchestration: A central "Orchestrator" service tells every other service exactly when to act and what to do. It's more complex to build but very easy to monitor and manage.

Conclusion

Mastering the Saga pattern allows you to maintain data consistency across dozens of independent microservices without the performance-killing overhead of traditional "Two-Phase Commit" protocols.
