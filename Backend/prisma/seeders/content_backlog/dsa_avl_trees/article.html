Understanding AVL Trees

An AVL tree is a self-balancing binary search tree (BST) where the difference between heights of left and right subtrees (the balance factor) cannot be more than one for all nodes.

1. Why Self-Balancing?

In a standard BST, operations like search and insertion can degrade to O(n) in the worst case (a 'skewed' tree). AVL trees maintain a height of O(log n), ensuring that these operations remain efficient even as the tree grows.

2. The Balance Factor

The Balance Factor is calculated as: Height(Left Subtree) - Height(Right Subtree). For an AVL tree, this value must be -1, 0, or 1.

3. Rotations

When an insertion or deletion breaks the balance, AVL trees use Rotations to restore it: Left Rotation, Right Rotation, Left-Right Rotation, and Right-Left Rotation.

Conclusion

AVL trees are foundational for applications requiring frequent data lookups and insertions while maintaining strict performance guarantees across all operations.
