Understanding Greedy Algorithms

A Greedy algorithm is an algorithmic paradigm that builds up a solution piece by piece, always choosing the next piece that offers the most immediate and obvious benefit.

1. The Greedy Choice Property

A global optimum can be reached by making a locally optimal (greedy) choice at each step. This means the algorithm never reconsiders its previous choices.

2. When to Use Greedy?

Greedy algorithms are typically used for optimization problems. However, they don't always produce the optimal solution unless the problem has Optimal Substructure and the Greedy Choice Property.

3. Classic Examples

Common greedy algorithms include Huffman Coding (for data compression), Dijkstra's Algorithm (shortest path), and Kruskal's/Prim's Algorithms (minimum spanning tree).

Conclusion

Greedy algorithms are often easy to implement and very efficient. While not always globally optimal, they provide excellent results for a wide range of important computational problems.
