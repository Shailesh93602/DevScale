Geospatial Design: Building a Global Uber Service

Designing a system like Uber or DoorDash requires solving a unique problem: Finding the "Nearest Driver" among millions of moving objects in real-time.

1. The Problem with Lat/Long

Standard SQL queries for "Nearest Location" (using math like the Haversine formula) are extremely slow when you have 1,000,000 drivers. You cannot scan every driver for every request.

2. The Solution: Geo-Hashing and Quad-Trees

  • Geo-Hashing: Dividing the world into small grid squares and giving each square a short ID (e.g., "gbsuv"). Drivers are indexed by their Square ID. To find a driver, you only look in the current square and its neighbors.
  • Quad-Trees: A hierarchical tree where each node has 4 children. If a square has too many drivers, it splits into 4 smaller squares. This allows the system to be "dense" in New York but "sparse" in the desert.

Conclusion

Mastering geospatial design allows you to build the world's most sophisticated real-world services. It teaches you to combine high-speed data structures with geographical logic for massive-scale efficiency.
