JavaScript Cookies: Storing Data in the Browser

In the early days of the web, **Cookies** were the only way to store small pieces of information in a user's browser. While we have newer and better tools today (like localStorage), cookies are still essential for handling sessions, logins, and tracking.

1. What is a Cookie?

A cookie is a small text file that a website can store on your computer. It can contain a unique "Session ID," your preferred language, or your "Add to Cart" items.

2. Using document.cookie

The original way to handle cookies in JavaScript. It can be a bit confusing because all cookies for your website are stored in one long, semicolor-separated string.

document.cookie = "username=EduScale; expires=Fri, 31 Dec 2026 12:00:00 UTC";

3. Secure Cookies

  • HttpOnly: Prevents JavaScript from reading the cookie (making it much harder for hackers to steal).
  • Secure: Ensures the cookie is only sent over an encrypted HTTPS connection.
  • SameSite: Prevents "Cross-Site Request Forgery" (CSRF) attacks by limiting which websites can access the cookie.

4. Cookies vs. LocalStorage

For most modern web applications, **LocalStorage** is a better choice than cookies for storing non-sensitive data because it's easier to use and has a much larger storage limit (about 5MB vs. 4KB for a cookie).

Reading Cookies (The Hard Way)

Since document.cookie is a long string, you often have to write a custom function to "parse" it and find the specific cookie you need. This is why many developers use a library like js-cookie.

💡 Pro Tip: If you're building a login system, always use **HttpOnly** and **Secure** cookies. This is the industry-standard way to protect your users' sensitive session information from malicious scripts.
